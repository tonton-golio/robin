# Fixer Signal

The most telling sign-up signal: **does this person fix or build things nobody asked them to?**

## The pattern

A new-volunteer chat asks people to bring something they've made or mended. Most bring an old project. A few make something new for the chat. Those who make something new — especially for the makerspace itself — get welcomed without hesitation.

**Case study**: [[riley-quinn|Riley Quinn]] and [[taylor-brooks|Taylor Brooks]] independently built little shelf organisers. Riley went further — a labelled, painted unit over a weekend. That was the moment the coordinator decided Riley was a keeper. Both joined; the unprompted build was the deciding signal.

## Where it shows up

- [[volunteer-assessment-portfolio]]: a `fixer_score` (1–5) is the top first-look score. Volume and initiative over polish.
- [[volunteer-rubric]]: "Fixer instinct" is one of the crew principles. Initiative is the first gate.
- [[sign-up-process]]: filtered for at every stage.

## Why it works

Compresses three qualities into one observable action:

1. **Initiative** — more than the minimum
2. **Taste** — picked a useful job, scoped it sensibly
3. **Follow-through** — finished something real in days

Someone who builds unprompted doesn't need to be nudged into doing things.

## Counter-examples

- **Applicant A**: blank portfolio. The chat confirmed little hands-on experience. Passed over.
- **Applicant B**: a half-done kit from a course — nothing made on their own. A concern.
- **Applicant C**: no projects to show despite past club work. A mild negative signal.

## Relationship to other signals

Necessary but not sufficient. One applicant had lots of notebooks of ideas (a weak fixer signal) but didn't work out on other grounds. [[sam-okafor]] impressed in the chat; the signal weighs differently by role.

[[distance-first-sizing]] came out of this same crew culture: the coordinator working out the sign layout in quiet sketching sessions rather than waiting for a brief.

## Implication for the sign-up bar

The current bar includes this signal. A volunteer who never fixes anything outside what's asked is a yellow flag — not a hard no, but it shifts the burden of proof. See [[volunteer-rubric]] for the full bar.
